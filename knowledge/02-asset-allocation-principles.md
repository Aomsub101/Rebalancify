# Asset Allocation Principles

## Overview

Asset allocation is the process of dividing an investment portfolio among different asset categories — such as equities, fixed income, real assets, and cash equivalents — with the goal of balancing risk and return in line with an investor's time horizon, risk tolerance, and financial objectives. Research consistently shows that asset allocation decisions account for the large majority of portfolio return variability over time, far outweighing the contribution of individual security selection or market timing.

The fundamental premise of asset allocation is that different asset classes behave differently under varying economic conditions. When equities are declining, bonds may be stable or rising. When both are under pressure, commodities or real estate may hold value. Spreading capital across these categories provides a more stable return stream than concentrating in any single one.

## Strategic Versus Tactical Asset Allocation

**Strategic asset allocation** establishes a long-term target mix — for example, 60% global equities and 40% investment-grade bonds — based on the investor's goals and risk tolerance. This target is reviewed infrequently (typically annually or after major life events) and forms the baseline from which all deviations are measured.

**Tactical asset allocation** involves short-term deviations from the strategic target in response to near-term market views. An investor might temporarily overweight equities if they expect strong economic growth, or underweight if they expect a recession. Tactical allocation requires skill, discipline, and the acceptance of being wrong. Academic evidence for the consistent profitability of tactical shifts is mixed; for most investors, maintaining the strategic target is superior to frequent tactical adjustments.

**Dynamic asset allocation** modifies the mix systematically in response to changing market conditions — for example, reducing equity exposure when valuations are high by historical measures. This approach sits between strategic and tactical allocation.

## Major Asset Classes and Their Characteristics

**Global equities** provide participation in corporate earnings growth and are the primary source of long-run real returns for most portfolios. Over long periods, equities have outperformed bonds and inflation. However, they are also the most volatile asset class in the short run and can experience sustained drawdowns of 40–60% or more.

**Investment-grade fixed income** (government bonds, high-quality corporate bonds) provides predictable income, acts as a portfolio stabiliser during equity market stress, and preserves capital in nominal terms. The trade-off is lower long-run returns. Duration risk — the sensitivity of bond prices to interest rate changes — must be managed carefully.

**High-yield fixed income** (sub-investment-grade corporate bonds) offers higher yields than investment-grade bonds but with credit risk closer to equities. High-yield bonds tend to be more correlated with equities than investment-grade bonds, limiting their diversification value.

**Real assets** — including real estate, infrastructure, and commodities — provide inflation protection, since their values and cash flows tend to rise with prices over time. Real estate and infrastructure also produce income. Commodities (gold, oil, agricultural products) have low or negative correlation with financial assets in some market regimes.

**Alternative assets** — hedge funds, private equity, private credit — can offer return streams with different risk profiles than public markets. Access is typically restricted to accredited investors, and liquidity is limited.

**Cash and short-term instruments** — money market funds, short-duration treasuries — have near-zero volatility in nominal terms but are subject to inflation erosion over time. They serve as a buffer for near-term spending needs and an opportunity fund.

## The Role of Time Horizon

Time horizon is arguably the most important determinant of the appropriate asset allocation. Investors with longer time horizons can tolerate short-term volatility, as they have time to recover from drawdowns. Investors with shorter horizons cannot afford sustained losses, since they may need to liquidate the portfolio before markets recover.

A common heuristic is to hold a higher equity allocation when young and gradually shift toward bonds and cash as the investor approaches the spending phase. Target-date funds implement this glide path automatically. The specific glide path is a function of the investor's unique circumstances, not a universal formula.

## Diversification Within Asset Classes

Asset allocation works at multiple levels. At the top level, it concerns the split between equities, bonds, and other broad categories. Within equities, further diversification across geographies (domestic, international developed, emerging markets) and sectors (technology, healthcare, financials, industrials) reduces concentration risk. Within fixed income, diversification across maturities and credit qualities reduces both duration and credit risk.

Geographic diversification is particularly valuable. Investors often hold a disproportionate share of their home country's assets — a phenomenon known as home bias. This leaves them exposed to country-specific risks (regulatory changes, political instability, currency depreciation) that could be diversified away by holding a globally representative portfolio.

## Rebalancing as Part of Allocation Management

Over time, asset classes grow at different rates, causing the actual allocation to drift away from the strategic target. A portfolio that began at 60/40 equities/bonds may drift to 70/30 after a prolonged equity bull market. This drift concentrates risk and should be corrected through periodic rebalancing — selling assets that have grown above their target and buying assets that have fallen below.

Rebalancing restores the intended risk profile, but also has a mechanistic contrarian effect: it forces selling of recent winners and buying of recent underperformers. Over long periods, this disciplined approach can add return relative to a drifting portfolio, though the benefit is not guaranteed.
