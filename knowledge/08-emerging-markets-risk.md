# Emerging Markets Risk

## Overview

Emerging markets (EM) are economies that are in a transitional phase between developing and developed status — characterised by rapidly growing but still relatively lower per-capita income, less mature capital markets, evolving institutional frameworks, and higher political and regulatory uncertainty compared to developed markets (DM). Major emerging markets include China, India, Brazil, South Korea, Taiwan, Mexico, Indonesia, South Africa, and Thailand, among many others.

For portfolio investors, emerging markets offer higher growth potential and diversification benefits, but come with a distinct risk profile that differs meaningfully from investing in developed markets. Understanding these specific risks is essential for sizing EM positions appropriately and anticipating scenarios that could impair returns.

## Economic Growth and the EM Investment Thesis

The core investment thesis for emerging markets is that faster economic growth relative to developed economies translates into faster corporate earnings growth, which over time is reflected in equity returns. EM countries have younger and growing workforces, lower per-capita income (implying greater room for catch-up growth), urbanisation trends, and large infrastructure investment programmes.

However, the link between GDP growth and equity market returns is weaker than intuition suggests. Much of EM growth accrues to private companies, governments, or workers rather than to listed public equity holders. Rapid growth can also be accompanied by high inflation, currency depreciation, and dilutive share issuance that offsets earnings gains. The historical record shows that the correlation between GDP growth rates and equity returns across countries is surprisingly low.

## Political and Governance Risk

Political risk is among the most significant and hardest-to-quantify risks in emerging markets. Sources of political risk include:

- **Policy reversal risk:** A new government may reverse privatisations, impose price controls, nationalise industries, or expropriate foreign assets. Sectors with regulatory dependence (utilities, banks, energy) are especially vulnerable.
- **Rule of law and corruption:** In countries with weak rule of law, minority shareholder rights may not be enforced. Contracts can be difficult to enforce. Corruption increases costs and uncertainty for businesses.
- **Geopolitical risk:** EM countries are more likely to be involved in regional conflicts or subject to international sanctions. Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022 led to the removal of Russian equities from EM indices — a total loss for investors.
- **Electoral risk:** Upcoming elections in EM countries can create significant market volatility, as candidates with heterodox economic policies can lead markets to reprice risk sharply.

## Currency Risk

Currency risk is one of the most impactful risks for foreign investors in EM assets. Returns earned in local currency must be converted back to the investor's home currency. If the EM currency depreciates against the investor's currency, it reduces returns or amplifies losses.

EM currencies are generally more volatile than DM currencies because:
- EM central banks have less credibility, making their commitments to price stability less reliable.
- EM economies are more dependent on commodity exports and global capital flows, both of which are volatile.
- EM countries with current account deficits are vulnerable to sudden stops — abrupt reversals of capital inflows that force currency devaluation.

Currency risk can be hedged, but hedging EM currencies is costly and sometimes impractical for smaller allocations.

## China Exposure and Concentration

China represents approximately 25–30% of major EM equity indices (MSCI Emerging Markets Index). This concentration means that EM index investors take significant single-country risk. Key China-specific risks include:

- **US-China geopolitical tension:** Trade disputes, technology export controls, and potential financial decoupling create regulatory risk for China-listed and US-listed Chinese ADRs.
- **Regulatory crackdowns:** The Chinese government's 2021 regulatory actions against the technology, education, and gaming sectors erased hundreds of billions in market value, demonstrating the extent to which Chinese companies are subject to government policy direction.
- **Taiwan risk:** A military conflict over Taiwan would have severe consequences for Asian markets broadly and Chinese equities specifically.
- **Variable interest entity (VIE) structure:** Many Chinese companies listed in the US are structured via VIEs — a legal arrangement that may not provide foreign investors with the rights they believe they hold.

Some institutional investors now separate China from the broad EM index, treating it as a distinct allocation.

## Liquidity Risk

EM markets tend to have lower trading volumes and wider bid-ask spreads than developed markets. During periods of global risk aversion, liquidity can deteriorate sharply as foreign investors withdraw capital simultaneously. This illiquidity can amplify drawdowns and make it difficult to rebalance or exit positions at reasonable prices during stress events.

## Inflation and Interest Rate Risk

Many EM economies have histories of high or volatile inflation. Central banks with less established credibility may be slower to tighten or may face political pressure not to raise rates aggressively. The result is that inflation can become embedded, eroding the real value of investments. High inflation also typically leads to higher nominal interest rates, which reduce bond values and increase borrowing costs for businesses.

## Practical Implications for Portfolio Construction

Given the above risks, most financial advisors recommend a modest EM allocation — typically 5–20% of the equity portion of the portfolio — for investors who have long time horizons and can tolerate higher volatility. Key principles:

- Use diversified EM funds rather than single-country exposure to avoid idiosyncratic country risk.
- Be aware of index concentration in China and consider whether separate China exposure management is appropriate.
- Size the EM allocation in proportion to the investor's risk tolerance and time horizon — EM volatility is typically 30–50% higher than DM equity volatility.
- Consider whether the portfolio's return objective genuinely requires EM exposure, or whether DM equities are sufficient.
