# Rebalancing Strategies

## Overview

Rebalancing is the process of realigning a portfolio's actual asset weights back to its target (strategic) allocation after market movements have caused the weights to drift. Without periodic rebalancing, a portfolio's risk profile changes over time as winning assets grow to represent a larger fraction of the total, increasing concentration and often increasing volatility.

There are several distinct approaches to rebalancing, each with different implications for trading frequency, transaction costs, tax efficiency, and the degree of drift tolerated. Choosing the right rebalancing strategy depends on the investor's costs, tax situation, and operational constraints.

## Calendar Rebalancing

Calendar rebalancing involves reviewing and restoring the target allocation at fixed intervals — monthly, quarterly, or annually. It is simple to implement and predictable, making it easy to automate or schedule. However, it is insensitive to the degree of drift: a portfolio might be rebalanced when drift is minimal (wasting transaction costs) or might tolerate large drift during the period between reviews.

Annual calendar rebalancing is a common choice for retail investors because it limits transaction costs and, in taxable accounts, can be aligned with tax planning. More frequent calendar rebalancing is generally justified only when assets are held in tax-advantaged accounts and transaction costs are negligible.

## Threshold Rebalancing (Tolerance Band)

Threshold rebalancing triggers a rebalancing action when any asset's weight drifts beyond a predefined tolerance band around its target. For example, if the equity target is 60%, a ±5% band would trigger rebalancing when equities reach 65% or 55% of the portfolio. The advantage is that rebalancing occurs only when it is materially needed, avoiding unnecessary trading in calm markets while responding promptly to large moves.

The trade-off is greater operational complexity: the portfolio must be monitored continuously or at least frequently. Common band widths range from 3% to 10% of portfolio value, depending on the asset's volatility and the investor's cost sensitivity.

**Relative vs. absolute bands:** An absolute band applies a fixed percentage point deviation (e.g., ±5pp). A relative band applies a percentage of the target (e.g., ±10% of the 60% target = ±6pp deviation allowed). Relative bands allow more drift for smaller allocations and less drift for larger ones, which can be more appropriate when the portfolio contains assets with very different target weights.

## Hybrid Rebalancing

Hybrid rebalancing combines calendar and threshold approaches. The portfolio is reviewed at fixed intervals, but rebalancing is only executed if any asset has drifted beyond its tolerance band. This captures the predictability of calendar rebalancing while avoiding unnecessary trades when drift is small.

Research by Vanguard and other institutions suggests that hybrid rebalancing with annual reviews and tolerance bands in the 5–10% range achieves most of the risk-management benefit of more frequent rebalancing, with materially lower trading costs.

## Buy-and-Hold Versus Rebalancing

In a pure buy-and-hold strategy, no rebalancing is performed. The portfolio drifts freely as markets evolve. In bull equity markets, this results in increasing equity concentration over time. The buy-and-hold approach avoids all rebalancing costs and can outperform in long, uninterrupted bull markets, but it increases the portfolio's risk profile and exposes the investor to a larger drawdown when equities eventually decline.

Academic evidence on whether rebalancing adds or subtracts return is mixed and depends heavily on the time period studied. What is clear is that rebalancing consistently controls risk: a rebalanced portfolio's actual volatility stays close to its intended level, while a drifting portfolio's volatility can increase substantially.

## Cash Flow Rebalancing

When an investor is regularly contributing to or withdrawing from a portfolio, rebalancing can be partially or fully achieved by directing cash flows toward underweight assets rather than selling overweight ones. This reduces turnover and, in taxable accounts, avoids triggering capital gains on appreciated positions.

Cash flow rebalancing is most effective when contribution amounts are large relative to portfolio size (common early in an investor's career) or when withdrawals are being taken systematically (common in retirement). As the portfolio grows relative to cash flows, explicit rebalancing trades become necessary to maintain alignment.

## Tax Considerations

Rebalancing involves selling appreciated assets, which can trigger capital gains taxes in taxable accounts. This is one of the most important practical constraints on rebalancing for retail investors with taxable portfolios.

Strategies to mitigate the tax cost of rebalancing include:

- **Asset location:** Hold assets expected to generate the most taxable events (high-turnover funds, rebalancing trades) in tax-advantaged accounts (pension, ISA, 401k, RRSP) and hold tax-efficient assets (buy-and-hold equity funds) in taxable accounts.
- **Tax-loss harvesting:** When markets decline, realise losses on underperforming assets to offset gains elsewhere in the portfolio. This can fund rebalancing trades without a net tax cost.
- **Rebalancing via contributions/withdrawals:** As described above, direct cash flows to avoid sales.
- **Delayed rebalancing in taxable accounts:** Accept slightly wider tolerance bands in taxable accounts to reduce the frequency of tax-generating trades.

## Transaction Costs and Minimum Trade Sizes

Small portfolio rebalancing trades can be disproportionately expensive as a percentage of the trade value, especially when brokerage commissions apply. Minimum trade sizes (e.g., only rebalance when the trade exceeds a certain dollar threshold) help avoid chasing small deviations with outsized costs.

Modern brokerage platforms offering commission-free trading have significantly reduced this concern for equity and ETF portfolios. However, bid-ask spreads remain a cost, particularly for less liquid assets.

## Partial vs. Full Rebalancing

Full rebalancing restores every asset exactly to its target weight. Partial rebalancing moves each asset partway toward its target — for example, closing 50% of the gap between actual and target weights. Partial rebalancing reduces turnover and costs while still reducing drift. It may be preferred in high-cost environments or when the rebalancing trigger is a smaller deviation.
