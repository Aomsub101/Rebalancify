# Behavioral Finance Biases

## Overview

Behavioural finance is the field that studies the influence of psychology on the financial decisions of investors and the consequences of these biases on financial markets. Traditional finance theory assumes that investors are rational agents who process information efficiently and make decisions that maximise their expected utility. Behavioural finance demonstrates that this assumption is systematically violated in predictable ways.

Understanding common cognitive and emotional biases is valuable for two reasons. First, it allows investors to recognise and correct for their own biases, potentially improving long-run portfolio outcomes. Second, it provides a framework for understanding why markets can misprice assets for extended periods — creating opportunities for disciplined investors.

## Loss Aversion

Loss aversion, identified by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky through prospect theory, describes the empirical finding that investors feel the pain of a loss approximately twice as intensely as they feel the pleasure of an equivalent gain. A $10,000 loss hurts about twice as much as a $10,000 gain feels good.

Loss aversion has several important portfolio implications:
- Investors hold losing positions too long, hoping to break even rather than accepting the loss and redeploying capital.
- Investors sell winning positions too early to lock in gains, even when the fundamental case for holding remains strong (the disposition effect).
- Investors accept lower expected returns on assets that feel "safe" and demand excessive compensation for assets that feel risky, distorting pricing.
- During market drawdowns, loss aversion causes investors to sell into falling markets, realising losses and missing subsequent recoveries.

## Overconfidence

Overconfidence is one of the most robust findings in behavioural finance. Investors systematically overestimate the accuracy of their knowledge, the reliability of their forecasts, and their ability to select outperforming investments.

**Calibration overconfidence:** When asked to set 90% confidence intervals for uncertain quantities, people's intervals contain the true answer only about 50% of the time. Investors believe they know more precisely than they do.

**Better-than-average effect:** Most investors believe they are above-average investors, which is mathematically impossible for the majority.

Overconfidence leads to excessive trading. Evidence from large brokerage samples (Barber and Odean, 2000) shows that investors who trade the most earn the lowest net returns, primarily due to transaction costs and poor timing. Overconfident investors mistake noise for signal and act on it.

## Confirmation Bias

Confirmation bias is the tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information in ways that confirm existing beliefs, while ignoring information that contradicts them. An investor who holds a strong positive view on a company will seek out bullish analyst reports, engage more with positive news, and discount negative developments.

Confirmation bias makes it harder for investors to update their views in response to new evidence. It can cause investors to hold concentrated positions long past the point where the thesis has changed, because they are not objectively processing contradictory information.

## Anchoring

Anchoring occurs when an investor gives disproportionate weight to an initial piece of information (the anchor) when making subsequent decisions. Common anchors include:

- **Purchase price:** Investors anchor to the price they paid for an asset and define gains and losses relative to it. This is psychologically meaningful but economically irrelevant — the relevant comparison is always the opportunity cost, not the historical cost.
- **52-week high/low:** Stocks near their 52-week low are often perceived as cheap even if the fundamentals have deteriorated.
- **Round numbers:** Psychological price targets at round numbers (1,000, 2,000 for indices) have no economic significance but influence investor behaviour.

## Herding

Herding describes the tendency of investors to follow the crowd, buying what others are buying and selling what others are selling. Herding can create momentum effects in prices — assets that are rising attract more buyers, driving them higher — but it can also cause bubbles when prices disconnect from fundamentals.

Individual investors herd for both information-based reasons (assuming others have superior information) and social reasons (fear of being wrong when everyone else is right, or of being alone when wrong). Institutional investors also herd due to career risk — deviating from consensus views exposes them to career-damaging underperformance.

## Mental Accounting

Mental accounting refers to the tendency to treat money differently depending on its source, its intended purpose, or its account location. An investor might treat money earned from gambling as "house money" and take more risks with it, while treating identical money earned from salary as precious.

Mental accounting causes investors to evaluate portfolio components in isolation rather than as part of an integrated whole. This leads to suboptimal diversification and inconsistent risk management.

## Recency Bias

Recency bias causes investors to overweight recent events when forming expectations about the future. After a prolonged bull market, investors assume strong returns will continue; after a crash, they expect further declines. This leads to the classic pattern of buying high (after prices have risen) and selling low (after prices have fallen) — the opposite of rational behaviour.

Recency bias is a primary driver of the "performance chasing" phenomenon, where investors move money into asset classes or funds that have recently performed well, typically near the peak of their cycle.

## Practical Implications for Portfolio Management

Awareness of these biases suggests several protective practices:

- Write down the investment thesis for each position and review it periodically against new evidence. If the thesis has changed, acknowledge it rather than rationalising continued holding.
- Define rules for rebalancing and stick to them mechanically, avoiding the temptation to override the rules based on recent market action.
- Diversify widely to reduce the emotional intensity of any single position's performance.
- Measure portfolio performance against appropriate benchmarks, not just against purchase price.
- Be especially sceptical of investment ideas that confirm existing beliefs — actively seek out counter-arguments.
