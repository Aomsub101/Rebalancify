# Fixed Income Basics

## Overview

Fixed income instruments are securities that pay the holder a defined series of cash flows — typically periodic interest payments (coupon payments) and a return of principal at maturity. Bonds are the most common fixed income instrument. They are issued by governments, government agencies, municipalities, and corporations to raise capital, and they are traded in secondary markets where their prices fluctuate with interest rates and credit conditions.

Fixed income is the second-largest asset class in global portfolios after equities. It plays a dual role: generating income and stabilising portfolio value during periods of equity market stress. Understanding the mechanics of bonds — how their prices relate to yields, how duration measures interest rate sensitivity, and how credit risk is priced — is essential for building and managing a multi-asset portfolio.

## Bond Mechanics: Price, Yield, and Coupon

A bond's **coupon rate** is the annual interest payment expressed as a percentage of its face (par) value. A bond with a $1,000 face value and a 5% coupon pays $50 per year, usually in semi-annual instalments of $25.

A bond's **yield to maturity (YTM)** is the discount rate that equates the present value of all future cash flows to the bond's current market price. YTM reflects the bond's expected annual return if held to maturity and all coupons are reinvested at the same rate. When a bond trades at par, its YTM equals its coupon rate. When a bond trades at a discount (below par), its YTM is higher than its coupon rate. When it trades at a premium (above par), its YTM is lower.

**Price and yield move inversely:** When interest rates rise, existing bonds with lower coupon rates become less attractive, so their prices fall to offer a competitive yield. When rates fall, existing bonds rise in price. This is the primary source of price risk in fixed income portfolios.

## Duration: Measuring Interest Rate Sensitivity

**Macaulay duration** is the weighted average time (in years) until a bond's cash flows are received, with weights equal to the present value of each cash flow as a fraction of the bond's total price. A longer Macaulay duration means the investor must wait longer on average for the bond's cash flows, and therefore the bond is more sensitive to interest rate changes.

**Modified duration** approximates the percentage change in a bond's price for a 1% (100 basis point) change in yield:

% Price Change ≈ −Modified Duration × Change in Yield

For example, a bond with a modified duration of 7 years will lose approximately 7% of its price if yields rise by 1 percentage point. Duration is therefore the primary tool for estimating and managing interest rate risk.

**Key duration relationships:**
- Longer maturity bonds have higher duration than shorter-maturity bonds with the same coupon.
- Zero-coupon bonds have duration equal to their maturity.
- Lower-coupon bonds have higher duration than higher-coupon bonds of the same maturity, since more of their value comes from the distant principal repayment.

**Convexity** is a second-order correction to the duration approximation. Bonds with higher convexity are less harmed by rising rates and benefit more from falling rates than the duration approximation suggests. Convexity is generally desirable and is priced accordingly.

## Credit Risk and Credit Ratings

Not all bonds carry the same probability of repayment. **Credit risk** is the risk that the bond issuer will fail to make a promised payment (default) or will be downgraded, causing the bond's price to decline.

Credit rating agencies (Moody's, S&P, Fitch) assess bond issuers and assign ratings along a scale. **Investment-grade bonds** are those rated BBB− or above by S&P (Baa3 or above by Moody's). **High-yield (junk) bonds** are rated below investment grade. Investment-grade bonds carry significantly lower default risk; high-yield bonds offer higher promised yields to compensate for their elevated credit risk.

**Spread** refers to the yield premium of a corporate or government bond over a benchmark risk-free bond (typically a government bond of similar maturity). Spreads widen during economic stress and tighten during good times. Monitoring spread levels is an important indicator of credit market sentiment.

**Sovereign bonds** — issued by national governments — are generally considered the lowest-risk bonds in their currency. However, even sovereign bonds can default: the risk depends on the government's fiscal position, debt sustainability, and whether it borrows in its own currency.

## Yield Curve Dynamics

The yield curve plots the yields of bonds of similar credit quality across different maturities. Its shape conveys important information about economic expectations and monetary policy.

**Normal (upward-sloping) curve:** Long-term yields are higher than short-term yields, reflecting the higher duration risk and uncertainty associated with lending for longer periods. This is the typical shape during economic expansion.

**Inverted yield curve:** Short-term yields exceed long-term yields. This shape has historically been a reliable predictor of recession, as it often signals that the central bank has raised short-term rates to a level that is expected to slow the economy.

**Flat yield curve:** Short and long-term yields are approximately equal, often a transitional shape seen during tightening or easing cycles.

## Types of Fixed Income Instruments

- **Government bonds:** Issued by national governments. In the US, these are Treasury bonds, notes, and bills. Considered the benchmark risk-free rate in their currency.
- **Investment-grade corporate bonds:** Issued by highly-rated companies. Offer higher yields than government bonds; exposed to both interest rate risk and credit risk.
- **High-yield corporate bonds:** Issued by lower-rated companies; yield premium compensates for higher default probability. Behaviour is more equity-like than investment-grade bonds.
- **Municipal bonds (US):** Issued by state and local governments. Income is often tax-exempt for US investors, making the tax-adjusted yield attractive for high-income investors.
- **Inflation-linked bonds:** Principal and coupon adjust with inflation (e.g., US TIPS, UK Gilts, French OATs). Protect against inflation eroding the real purchasing power of bond returns.
- **Mortgage-backed securities (MBS):** Pools of residential mortgages securitised into tradable bonds. Subject to prepayment risk — homeowners may refinance early, altering the expected cash flow timing.
