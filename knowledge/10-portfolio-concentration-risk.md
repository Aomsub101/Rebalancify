# Portfolio Concentration Risk

## Overview

Portfolio concentration risk refers to the danger that too much of a portfolio's value is exposed to a single asset, sector, geography, or risk factor. A concentrated portfolio can generate exceptional returns when the concentrated positions perform well, but it exposes the investor to devastating losses when they do not. The core principle of diversification — that combining imperfectly correlated assets reduces portfolio volatility below the weighted average of individual asset volatilities — argues against concentration.

Understanding concentration risk requires distinguishing between different types of concentration, how each is measured, and when concentration may be justified or unjustified.

## Types of Concentration

**Single-asset concentration** occurs when a large fraction of portfolio value is in one security. This is the most extreme form of concentration and the hardest to justify for a long-term investor. A single company can fail, be regulated out of existence, commit fraud, or simply decline for structural competitive reasons — while the rest of the market is doing well. The historical list of once-dominant companies that were poor long-run investments is long (Eastman Kodak, Enron, Nokia, General Electric at peak).

**Sector concentration** occurs when the portfolio is overweight in a single industry group relative to the market. A portfolio heavily concentrated in technology stocks in 1999, in financials in 2007, or in energy in 2014 would have suffered severe losses during the subsequent sector downturns, even if the holdings were diversified across companies within the sector.

**Geographic concentration** (home bias) is the tendency to hold a disproportionately large share of assets from one's home country. Investors in smaller markets are especially prone to this: a Thai investor holding 80% Thai equities is exposed to Thailand-specific political and economic risks that are uncorrelated with global growth. Home bias reduces diversification at the portfolio level.

**Factor concentration** occurs when a portfolio is heavily exposed to a single risk factor (e.g., value, momentum, quality). Even well-defined factors can underperform for a decade or more. A portfolio that is implicitly or explicitly concentrated in a single factor magnifies the bet on that factor's continued premium.

**Counterparty concentration** is less commonly considered but equally important: holding cash at a single bank, using a single brokerage, or depending on a single custodian concentrates institutional risk. Bank failures and brokerage insolvencies, while uncommon in regulated markets, do occur.

## Measuring Concentration

Several metrics quantify portfolio concentration:

**Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI):** Calculated as the sum of squared portfolio weights. A portfolio with 100 equal-weight assets would have HHI = 100 × (0.01)² = 0.01. A portfolio with 50% in one asset would have much higher HHI. Higher HHI indicates greater concentration.

**Effective number of holdings:** The reciprocal of HHI, expressed as a number. A diversified 100-asset portfolio has an effective count near 100. A concentrated portfolio has an effective count well below its actual number of holdings.

**Maximum single-name weight:** A simple ceiling on any individual asset's weight. Common institutional guidelines include 5% per name, 25% per sector, and 40% per region.

**Tracking error:** For a portfolio measured against a benchmark, tracking error measures how differently the portfolio performs from the index. High tracking error implies concentrated active bets.

## When Concentration Can Be Justified

Concentration is not inherently wrong — the cost is justified when the investor has genuine informational or analytical edge. Warren Buffett and other concentrated value investors have demonstrated that concentration in a small number of deeply researched positions can outperform diversified index funds over long periods. However, this outperformance is conditional on:

- Superior understanding of the businesses held
- Emotional fortitude to endure extended periods of underperformance
- A long time horizon that allows the investment thesis to play out
- Positions sized such that even catastrophic outcomes in individual positions don't threaten financial survival

For the vast majority of investors who lack significant research resources and who may need to access their capital before a long investment thesis is vindicated, concentrated portfolios are hard to justify. The opportunity cost of concentration — surrendering the free diversification benefit — requires a high degree of conviction and skill.

## Concentration Risk in Employee Stock Ownership

Employees who hold significant employer stock — through ESOPs, equity compensation, or historical accumulation — face a compounded concentration risk. Their human capital (future earnings) is already concentrated in the same entity as their financial capital. A company failure would destroy both simultaneously, which is the worst possible correlation from a portfolio perspective.

Classic examples include Enron employees who held retirement savings heavily in Enron stock: the company's collapse eliminated both their jobs and their retirement savings simultaneously. Financial planning guidance universally recommends limiting employer stock to a small fraction of investable assets regardless of the employee's confidence in the company.

## Practical Concentration Limits

Common practical guidelines for retail investors:

- No single equity security should exceed 5–10% of the total portfolio.
- No single sector should exceed 25% of the equity allocation (or 25% more than the market weight in that sector).
- No single country beyond the home country should exceed 15% of the equity allocation without strong justification.
- Non-standard assets (individual bonds, private placements, illiquid instruments) should generally be sized below 5% each.

These limits are not laws — they are defaults appropriate for investors who do not have specialised expertise. Informed, research-supported deviation from these guidelines is reasonable; uninformed concentration based on past performance, familiarity, or overconfidence is not.
