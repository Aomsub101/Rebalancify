# Crypto Asset Characteristics

## Overview

Cryptographic digital assets — commonly called cryptocurrencies or crypto assets — are a novel asset class that emerged with the publication of the Bitcoin whitepaper by Satoshi Nakamoto in 2008. They are digital bearer instruments secured by cryptographic proofs on decentralised distributed ledgers (blockchains). Their characteristics differ substantially from traditional asset classes and create both distinctive opportunities and risks.

Understanding crypto assets requires separating their monetary and speculative characteristics, their technical architecture, and the evolving regulatory environment. For portfolio managers, the most important questions concern return and volatility patterns, correlation with traditional assets, liquidity, and the appropriate position sizing given their risk profile.

## Blockchain Architecture

All crypto assets are built on distributed ledger technology. Transactions are grouped into blocks, which are chained together using cryptographic hashes. Each block references the hash of the previous block, making the chain immutable once written — altering a historical block would invalidate all subsequent blocks.

**Consensus mechanisms** determine how the network reaches agreement on valid transactions:
- **Proof of Work (PoW):** Miners compete to solve a computationally intensive puzzle. The winner adds the next block and receives a reward. Bitcoin uses PoW. It is energy-intensive but has proven secure over 15+ years.
- **Proof of Stake (PoS):** Validators stake (lock up) their own tokens as collateral. They are selected to propose the next block in proportion to their stake. Ethereum switched to PoS in 2022. PoS is far more energy-efficient but introduces different security trade-offs.

## Bitcoin

Bitcoin (BTC) is the first and largest cryptocurrency by market capitalisation. It operates on a fixed monetary policy: the total supply is capped at 21 million coins, and new coins are issued through mining rewards that halve approximately every four years (halving events). This programmatic scarcity is central to Bitcoin's value proposition as a store of value and potential inflation hedge.

Bitcoin has no central issuer or controlling entity. Its rules are enforced by the decentralised network of nodes running the Bitcoin protocol. Major changes require broad consensus among network participants.

From a portfolio perspective, Bitcoin has historically exhibited:
- Very high volatility relative to equities (annualised standard deviation often 60–100%)
- Low or modest correlation with equities during normal markets, but higher correlation during acute risk-off events
- Significant drawdowns (50–80%) in bear market cycles
- Strong absolute returns over sufficiently long horizons when bought outside of peak bubbles

## Ethereum and Smart Contract Platforms

Ethereum (ETH) is the second-largest crypto asset and the dominant smart contract platform. Smart contracts are self-executing programs stored on the blockchain that automatically enforce the terms of agreements without a trusted intermediary. Ethereum's programmability enabled the development of decentralised finance (DeFi), non-fungible tokens (NFTs), and decentralised applications (dApps).

Ethereum's monetary policy is more complex than Bitcoin's. Following the 2022 Merge to PoS, new ETH issuance is significantly lower and burning of transaction fees (EIP-1559) means that during periods of high activity, ETH can be deflationary. The investment thesis for ETH centres on its role as the "gas" powering the world's largest programmable blockchain.

## Altcoins and the Risk Spectrum

Beyond Bitcoin and Ethereum, thousands of alternative cryptocurrencies (altcoins) exist. These range from established Layer 1 blockchains (Solana, Avalanche, Cardano) to decentralised finance protocols, gaming tokens, and meme coins. The risk spectrum is wide:

- Established large-cap altcoins (top 10 by market cap) have high liquidity and some institutional adoption, but greater volatility than BTC or ETH.
- Mid-cap and small-cap tokens have very thin liquidity, extreme volatility, and higher probability of going to zero.
- Meme coins (e.g., Dogecoin, Shiba Inu) have minimal intrinsic utility and are driven primarily by speculation and social momentum.

For most portfolio investors, exposure beyond the top 2–3 assets by market cap represents speculative rather than strategic allocation.

## Stablecoins

Stablecoins are crypto tokens designed to maintain a fixed value relative to a fiat currency (usually the US dollar). They serve as the settlement layer of DeFi protocols and as a way to maintain crypto exposure without conversion to fiat. Key types:

- **Fiat-backed stablecoins** (USDC, USDT): Each token is backed by reserves of fiat currency or short-term government securities held by a centralised custodian. Risk: custodian failure or reserve misrepresentation (see Tether controversies).
- **Crypto-backed stablecoins** (DAI): Overcollateralised with crypto assets held in smart contracts. Risk: collateral value collapse during crypto market crashes.
- **Algorithmic stablecoins:** Maintain peg through algorithmic supply adjustment rather than reserves. UST/Luna's collapse in May 2022, which erased $40 billion of value, demonstrated the extreme fragility of this approach.

## Portfolio Implications

Including a small allocation to crypto assets in a diversified portfolio is debated among institutional investors. Arguments for inclusion:

- Low long-run correlation with equities and bonds provides diversification.
- Asymmetric return profile: small allocations limit downside while preserving exposure to potential large gains.
- Potential inflation hedge for Bitcoin, though empirical evidence from 2021–2022 was inconsistent.

Arguments against or for caution:

- Very high volatility means even a small position (2–5%) can contribute disproportionately to portfolio volatility.
- Correlation with equities has increased during market stress events.
- Regulatory uncertainty remains high in many jurisdictions.
- Custody and key management risk is unique to crypto: loss of private keys means permanent loss of assets.

Position sizing recommendations typically range from 0–5% of portfolio for investors who wish to include crypto, depending on risk tolerance and conviction.
