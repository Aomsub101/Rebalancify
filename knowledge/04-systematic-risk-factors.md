# Systematic Risk Factors

## Overview

Factor investing is a systematic approach to portfolio construction that seeks to capture documented, persistent return premia by targeting specific characteristics (factors) that explain the cross-section of asset returns. Rather than relying on individual security selection, factor investing tilts a portfolio toward broad groups of stocks or bonds that share certain attributes associated with higher long-run expected returns.

The theoretical basis for factor premia is either risk-based (investors require compensation for bearing a specific type of risk) or behavioural (systematic investor errors create persistent mispricings). Understanding the source of a factor's premium helps assess how durable it is likely to be going forward.

## The Fama-French Three-Factor Model

The most influential factor model in academic finance was developed by Eugene Fama and Kenneth French in 1992–1993. It extends the Capital Asset Pricing Model's single-factor (market beta) framework with two additional factors:

**Market (MKT):** The excess return of the broad market over the risk-free rate. All diversified equity portfolios have significant market exposure. This is the dominant driver of equity returns over time.

**Size (SMB — Small Minus Big):** Small-capitalisation stocks have historically earned higher returns than large-cap stocks on a risk-adjusted basis. The premium is attributed to both illiquidity risk (small caps are harder to trade) and to the fact that small companies are more economically sensitive. The size premium is well-documented but has been modest in recent decades.

**Value (HML — High Minus Low):** Value stocks — those with low price-to-book, low price-to-earnings, or similar valuation multiples — have historically outperformed growth stocks over long horizons. The premium is attributed to financial distress risk (cheap stocks are often distressed) and to behavioural overreaction (investors overpay for glamour growth stocks and underpay for boring value stocks).

## The Fama-French Five-Factor Extension

In 2015, Fama and French extended the model to five factors:

**Profitability (RMW — Robust Minus Weak):** Companies with higher operating profitability earn higher returns. Profitable companies tend to be competitively advantaged and have lower financial distress risk.

**Investment (CMA — Conservative Minus Aggressive):** Companies that invest conservatively (low asset growth) outperform companies that invest aggressively. Overinvestment is often associated with managerial overconfidence or poor capital allocation.

The five-factor model substantially improves the explanation of cross-sectional return differences compared to the three-factor model.

## The Momentum Factor

Momentum — the tendency of recent winners to continue outperforming and recent losers to continue underperforming over a horizon of 6–12 months — is one of the most robust factors empirically. Momentum was documented by Jegadeesh and Titman in 1993 and has been replicated across asset classes and geographies.

The momentum premium is generally attributed to behavioural causes: investors underreact to new information, causing trends to persist longer than they should. Momentum is also particularly susceptible to sharp reversals during market crashes, which is a significant risk for momentum strategies.

## Low Volatility / Minimum Variance

Empirically, low-volatility stocks have earned returns similar to or higher than high-volatility stocks, contradicting the CAPM prediction that higher risk should yield higher return. This "volatility anomaly" is attributed to several behaviours: institutional investors benchmarked to market indices avoid low-volatility stocks; retail investors overpay for lottery-like high-volatility stocks; leverage constraints prevent arbitrage.

Low-volatility strategies have historically provided strong risk-adjusted returns and significant downside protection.

## Quality Factor

Quality can be defined in multiple ways — high profit margins, low earnings variability, strong balance sheets, high return on equity — but consistently high-quality companies have outperformed over time. Quality partly overlaps with the profitability factor in the five-factor model.

## Factor Cyclicality and Diversification

Individual factors can underperform for extended periods (a decade or more) even if their long-run premium is genuine. Value underperformed severely in the decade following the 2008 financial crisis; small cap had a prolonged underperformance period in the 1990s. Investors who target a single factor face meaningful implementation risk.

Combining multiple low-correlated factors (value + momentum + quality + low-vol) can reduce factor timing risk. Factors that are negatively correlated with each other — particularly value and momentum, which tend to diverge — provide diversification benefits when combined.

## Factor Exposure in Practice

Factor exposure can be achieved in multiple ways:

- **Smart beta ETFs:** Passively rebalanced funds that screen or weight by factor scores. These are transparent, liquid, and low-cost. Examples include value ETFs (IVE, VTV), quality ETFs (QUAL), low-volatility ETFs (USMV, SPLV), and multifactor ETFs (QMOM, OMFL).
- **Active factor funds:** Discretionary or systematic funds that target multiple factors with proprietary implementations. Typically higher cost but potentially more precise.
- **Direct indexing:** Building a custom portfolio of individual securities to target specific factor exposures, while allowing tax-loss harvesting at the individual security level.

## Risks of Factor Investing

- **Crowding:** Popular factor strategies can become crowded as capital flows in, compressing future premia.
- **Factor timing difficulty:** Timing when a factor will revert to performance after an extended underperformance is extremely difficult.
- **Definition risk:** Different providers define factors differently. A value ETF from one provider may have very different characteristics from another.
- **Rebalancing costs:** Factor portfolios require frequent turnover to maintain factor purity, which generates transaction costs.
