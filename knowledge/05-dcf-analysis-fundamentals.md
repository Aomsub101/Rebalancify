# DCF Analysis Fundamentals

## Overview

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) analysis is a valuation method that estimates the intrinsic value of an asset based on the present value of its expected future cash flows. The fundamental premise is that a dollar received today is worth more than a dollar received in the future, because today's dollar can be invested to earn a return. DCF analysis formalises this time value of money principle into a systematic framework for valuing businesses, projects, and individual securities.

When an asset's market price is below its estimated intrinsic value from DCF analysis, it may be considered undervalued. When the price is above intrinsic value, it may be overvalued. DCF is therefore a tool for identifying potential investment opportunities, though it requires carefully considered assumptions and has important limitations.

## Core Formula

The DCF value of an asset is the sum of all expected future cash flows, each discounted to present value at an appropriate discount rate:

DCF Value = CF1 / (1 + r)¹ + CF2 / (1 + r)² + CF3 / (1 + r)³ + ... + CFn / (1 + r)ⁿ + TV / (1 + r)ⁿ

Where:
- CFt = cash flow expected in period t
- r = discount rate (reflects the riskiness of the cash flows)
- n = number of explicit forecast periods
- TV = terminal value at the end of the forecast period

In practice, analysts explicitly forecast cash flows for a finite period (typically 5–10 years) and then estimate a terminal value representing the value of all cash flows beyond the forecast horizon.

## Free Cash Flow

The most common cash flow measure used in DCF analysis is Free Cash Flow to the Firm (FCFF), also called unlevered free cash flow:

FCFF = EBIT × (1 - Tax Rate) + Depreciation & Amortisation − Capital Expenditures − Changes in Working Capital

FCFF represents the cash generated by the business that is available to all capital providers — both debt holders and equity holders — before debt service. Using FCFF with a weighted-average cost of capital (WACC) as the discount rate produces the enterprise value of the firm.

Alternatively, Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE) adjusts for debt cash flows and is discounted at the cost of equity to produce the equity value directly.

## Discount Rate Selection

The discount rate is the most sensitive input in a DCF model. A higher discount rate reduces present values; a lower rate increases them. The appropriate rate reflects the riskiness of the cash flows being discounted.

**Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC):** For firm valuation, WACC blends the cost of equity and the after-tax cost of debt, weighted by their proportions in the capital structure. WACC is used to discount FCFF.

**Cost of Equity:** Typically estimated using CAPM: Cost of Equity = Risk-Free Rate + β × Equity Risk Premium. The risk-free rate is usually the yield on long-term government bonds. The equity risk premium varies by market but is often estimated at 4–6%.

**Cost of Debt:** The yield on the company's existing debt, adjusted for the tax shield (interest is tax-deductible): After-Tax Cost of Debt = Pre-Tax Yield × (1 - Tax Rate).

## Terminal Value

The terminal value captures the bulk of DCF value — in many analyses, it represents 60–80% of the total estimate. There are two common approaches:

**Gordon Growth Model (Perpetuity Growth):** TV = FCFFn × (1 + g) / (WACC - g), where g is the long-run sustainable growth rate. This rate should not exceed the long-run GDP growth rate, as no company can grow faster than the economy indefinitely. Common values are 2–3% for mature businesses in developed markets.

**Exit Multiple Method:** Applies a market-observed multiple (e.g., EV/EBITDA) to the forecast terminal-year metric to estimate the value at which the business could be sold.

Both approaches require judgement and are sensitive to their key assumptions.

## Sensitivity Analysis

Because DCF outcomes depend heavily on assumptions about growth rates, margins, and discount rates, sensitivity analysis is essential. A two-way table showing how intrinsic value changes across a range of growth rates and WACC values reveals how confident an investor should be in the estimate.

A high-quality DCF analysis quantifies the range of plausible intrinsic values, not just a single point estimate. If the current market price falls well below even the pessimistic end of the range, the margin of safety is large. If the price is at or above the optimistic estimate, the asset is fully or richly valued.

## Common Pitfalls in DCF Analysis

- **Garbage in, garbage out:** DCF results are only as reliable as the input assumptions. Overly optimistic growth forecasts are the most common error.
- **Terminal value dominance:** When 70%+ of value comes from the terminal value, small changes in the perpetuity growth rate or exit multiple drive huge changes in the result. Investors should be especially cautious about terminal value assumptions.
- **Circular WACC:** WACC depends on the market value of equity, which is what the DCF is trying to estimate. Iteration is required.
- **Ignoring working capital and capex:** Revenue and earnings growth often require proportional increases in working capital and capital expenditure. Models that project revenue growth without corresponding capital requirements overstate FCF.
- **Currency and inflation inconsistency:** Nominal cash flows must be discounted at a nominal rate; real cash flows at a real rate. Mixing the two produces errors.

## Relative Valuation as a Complement

DCF is best used alongside relative valuation — comparing price multiples (P/E, EV/EBITDA, P/FCF) to peers and historical averages. If both a DCF and peer multiples analysis point to undervaluation, conviction is higher. When they conflict, the reason for the discrepancy is worth investigating.
